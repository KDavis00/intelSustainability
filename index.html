1960s

The dawn of computing, with foundational innovations that would later enable energy efficiency.

1968

Intel Founded

Intel was founded by Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore to pursue bold ideas in computing.

1969

Intel's First Product

Intel released its first product, the 3101 Schottky RAM, generating revenue while engineers developed metal-oxide semiconductors.

The 1101

Intel released the 1101 static RAM, the first commercial chip using metal-oxide semiconductors and silicon gates. These innovations improved efficiency and cost, paving the way for PCs.

1970s

Intel grew into a leading tech company, introducing the first programmable microprocessor and making computing affordable.

1971

First Company-Owned Facility

In 1971, Intel opened its first company-owned manufacturing facility in Santa Clara, California. This marked the beginning of Intel's expansion and set the stage for future environmental considerations.

First Microprocessor

Intel debuts the 4004, the world's first commercial microprocessor.

1972

Introduction of the 1103 DRAM

Intel introduced the 1103, the world's first commercially available dynamic RAM (DRAM) chip. This innovation significantly improved memory storage efficiency, indirectly contributing to energy savings in electronic devices.

1973-1975

Expansion and Technological Advancements

Intel expanded its product line and manufacturing capabilities while focusing on key technological advancements. This growth laid the foundation for future sustainability efforts by increasing its capacity to produce more energy-efficient products.

1976

Introduction of the 8080

Intel introduced the 8080 microprocessor which became the foundation for many early personal computers. The development of such energy-efficient processors contributed to reducing the overall energy consumption of computing devices.

1978

8086 Processor

Intel released the 8086 microprocessor. It was a 16-bit processor, a significant advancement over the earlier 8-bit processors.The 8086’s architecture enabled more efficient computing, which later aligned with Intel’s sustainability goals.

1980s

1980: Intel expanded global operations; environmental considerations were minimal but energy and resource tracking began informally.

1981-1983

Energy Management and the 386 Processor

Intel refined its chip manufacturing processes to reduce material waste and optimize energy use. Improvements in factory operations lowered scrap rates and electricity consumption.

1985

Under CEO Andy Grove, Intel made the pivotal, and radical, decision to exit the DRAM market. This strategic shift allowed the company to focus its resources on microprocessors, leading to the development of the groundbreaking 386 processor

386 Processor

The 386 was built with a higher density of transistors (over 275,000) than any previous chip. To make production economically viable, Intel had to dramatically improve its manufacturing yields, which meant producing more good chips per silicon wafer. This focus on efficiency naturally reduced the amount of raw materials, water, and energy wasted on producing defective products.

1988

Environmental Initiatives Begin

Intel implemented a "smoke-free" policy, a step that, while not directly related to industrial pollution, showed an early, if limited, awareness of corporate responsibility for health and public perception.

2000s

The company intensifies its focus on environmental stewardship and corporate social responsibility.

2000

First Public Report

Intel publishes its first public Corporate Responsibility Report, providing transparency on its environmental performance.

2006

Peak GHG Emissions

Intel reaches its highest annual greenhouse gas emissions and begins reductions.

2010s

Renewable energy commitments and responsible sourcing become a top priority for Intel.

2010

New Goals

Intel set a new public goal to source 100% of its electricity from renewable sources by 2030. This ambitious target became a central focus of its climate strategy.The company intensified its focus on water conservation, a critical issue for the water-intensive semiconductor industry, especially in drought-prone regions where many of its facilities are located. Intel began investing heavily in water reclamation and recycling systems.

2012

Conflict-Free Minerals

Intel declares its microprocessors to be conflict-free, a milestone in supply chain ethics and sustainability.

Renewable Energy Leadership

Intel was the largest voluntary corporate purchaser of renewable energy in the U.S., using over 2.5 billion kWh annually — 88% of its nationwide electricity.

2014

Water Recycling

ntel's factories began to implement highly advanced water conservation measures, with some sites achieving near-100% recycling rates within specific manufacturing processes. This initiative was part of Intel's broader commitment to reducing its environmental footprint. By investing in on-site water reclamation and recycling facilities, Intel was able to drastically reduce its reliance on local freshwater supplies, laying the foundation for its ambitious future goals.

2020s

Intel sets ambitious long-term goals for carbon neutrality and sustainable operations.

2020

RISE Strategy

Intel launched its RISE strategy (Responsible, Inclusive, and Sustainable world Enabled), which serves as a comprehensive framework for its corporate responsibility efforts. The sustainability component of the strategy is focused on climate, water, and waste.

2022

Net-Zero By 2040

A major milestone was reached when Intel pledged to achieve net-zero Scope 1 and 2 greenhouse gas emissions by 2040. This commitment placed Intel among the more aggressive climate leaders in the tech industry. The company also announced a goal to achieve net-zero upstream Scope 3 emissions by 2050, acknowledging the environmental impact of its entire supply chain.

2023

Renewable Electricity

Intel achieved a significant milestone, reaching 99% renewable electricity use worldwide. This accomplishment was a result of its long-term strategy to invest in a portfolio of renewable energy sources, including direct purchases, on-site generation, and renewable energy credits. This progress puts the company well on track to meet its goal of sourcing 100% of its global electricity from renewable sources by 2030.

2024 and onward

Sustainability Summit

Intel hosts its first Sustainability Summit, uniting suppliers, government officials, and industry leaders to collaborate on next-generation sustainable semiconductor manufacturing.

To the future

Intel continues to make progress toward its 2030 goals. It is currently at 98% global renewable electricity and has achieved net positive water in key locations like the U.S., Mexico, Costa Rica, and India. The company also focuses on waste, with a target of zero waste to landfill by 2030.
